Province Overview

This page gives an overview of the current state of public transportation categorized for each province. It will provide more insight into several topics, including population and busstops per inhabitant.

Population

The graphs above show how the population of the Netherlands is divided across the twelve different provinces. It is clearly visible from the choropleth on the left that most people live in the west. The pie chart on the right shows what percentage of the total population lives in each province.

Population per km2

Looking at the number of inhabitants per square kilometer, we find that population density is also highest in the west of the country. The three provinces that stand out are: Zuid-Holland, Noord-Holland and Utrecht. These three provinces combined are known as the Randstad. It contains the four biggest cities of the Netherlands. We will be comparing these provinces with the other, less densely populated provinces.

Total Amount of Bus Stops

Looking at the total amount of bus stops in each province, it seems to be divided according to the population: when comparing this choropleth map and pie chart to their counterparts from the 'total population' section above, they look very much alike. This might give the impression that bus stops are divided very well among the different provinces, but when we look at relative instead of absolute statistics, we see a different picture, as shown below.

Bus Stops per Inhabitant

Looking at the density of bus stops compared to each province's number of inhabitants, we find a map that is almost the opposite of what we have seen so far. Because the provinces outside of the Randstad are less densely populated, there are actually more bus stops available per inhabitant. Another reason for this could be that a lot of cities in the Randstad have subway or tram networks and one or more train stations in addition to their bus networks, which is not the case outside of the Randstad. One clear outlier is Flevoland: though its population is comparable to that of Drenthe or Zeeland, it has by far the least amount of bus stops available for its inhabitants, both absolutely and relatively. A report on the website of the European Commission from 2006 indicated that at that time, Flevoland was already lagging behind the other provinces. It seems that 8 years later, there is still a lot of room for improvement.
